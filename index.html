An Interview with Bosch’s Wu Yongqiao: 6 Insights to Clear the Fog and Fuel the Global Breakthrough of Smart Cockpits

 

AI Cockpit has entered a new phase of AI-driven development, with large models and multimodal interaction technologies reshaping the Whole Vehicle logic. As Chinese automakers expand globally, varying regulations, data governance policies, and user habits across markets have turned the development of globally deployable Cockpit solutions into a critical industry challenge.

 

 

At the 2026 Qualcomm Automotive Technologies and Cooperation Summit, this topic sparked industry discussion. As a major player in the global AI Cockpit market, Wu Yongqiao, Regional President of Bosch Cross-Domain Computing Solutions China, drawing from Bosch's experience of delivering more than 10 million AI Cockpit products worldwide, shared six key insights into the future development of AI Cockpit. In his view, the competitive edge of the AI Cockpit industry lies in the companies’ ability to systematically overcome a range of complex challenges, including global adaptation, privacy security, computing efficiency, legal compliance, business models, and engineering implementation.

 

 

In an in-depth conversation with Global Auto, Wu Yongqiao explored these topics further, seeking to look beyond the current wave of technological enthusiasm and identify the path toward globally scalable AI Cockpit.

 

Level 3 Autonomous Driving Is a Prerequisite for AI Cockpit Takeoff, instead of a Bonus Feature

Global Auto: The industry is increasingly focusing on the "third space," with many automakers showcasing future in-vehicle scenarios such as mobile offices, entertainment, and even rest areas. Yet you have described the slow progress of Level 3 autonomous driving regulations as the "Damocles sword" hanging over the AI Cockpit industry. What is the reason behind this?

 

Wu Yongqiao: Without large-scale Level 3 deployment, the "third space" remains a mirage. This is not a technology issue—it is a question of liability and the allocation of responsibility.

 

For years, the industry has long sought to transform the automobile from mere transportation tools to mobile living space. However, the prerequisite for such scenarios is that the driver must be substantially freed from driving tasks. In reality, mainstream ADAS Solutions products on the market today remain at L2 to L2+ assisted driving levels, where driving responsibility still fully rests with the driver.

 

This creates the fundamental underlying contradiction in the AI Cockpit industry: on one hand, AI Cockpits are racing toward "immersive experiences," while on the other, drivers must remain fully attentive and ready to take over at any moment. The misalignment between experience and responsibility means the so-called "third space", at this stage, remains largely a conceptual construct rather than a mature, consumer-ready product. Even with rich entertainment or office features, drivers still needs to monitor the road, making it impossible to comfortably enjoy these AI Cockpit features.

This issue is a global challenge. Across overseas markets, regulatory frameworks, liability allocation, and insurance systems for Level 3 autonomous driving are still incomplete, which limit the real-world deployment of AI Cockpit scenarios.

 

Technology determines whether a function can be realized, while regulation determines whether products can be large-scale adopted. Only when Level 3 regulatory frameworks are fully established, allowing consumers to truly set their driving concerns aside, will the AI Cockpit industry enter a phase of acceleration and breakout.

 

 

The Invisible Threshold to Mass Production of Large Models: Control AI Hallucinations First, Then Discuss Experience Upgrades

Global Auto: Many AI Cockpit models perform brilliantly in laboratory testing. However, once they are mass-produced and installed in vehicles, numerous issues come up. You've mentioned that the "vehicle Harness system is an invisible threshold that must be crossed before mass production." What specific problem does this system solve, and why is it so important?

 

Wu Yongqiao: No matter how well large models perform in the lab, that doesn't equate to reliable in-vehicle experience. The number one enemy of in-vehicle large models is AI Hallucination.

 

Laboratory environments are simple and controllable and cannot reflect complex real-world road conditions. Large models are prone to "AI hallucination", such as comprehensive deviation or generating incorrect instructions. Once such outputs are used to control a vehicle, they can directly threaten driving safety. For example, when a user says while driving on the highway at night, "It's a bit dazzling, adjust the lights," the model mistakenly interprets this as turning off the headlights instead of switching between high and low beams. The vehicle could suddenly lose illumination at high speed. Similarly, if a user says, "I feel stuffy, open the window a little," and the model, due to contextual deviation, lowers all windows fully—which could create safety risks in heavy rain or at high speeds.

 

The vehicle Harness system is essentially a safety governance and command verification framework for in-vehicle AI. It is the safety firewall for mass production deployment, an invisible threshold that every large model must cross before being installed in vehicles.

 

At the implementation level, large models must comply with two mandatory standards: First, high-risk control commands such as window adjustment, driving mode switching, and emergency alerts must undergo structured processing + dual confirmation. Second, a shadow-mode trial must be implemented, where new features run in the background without directly controlling hardware. Only after achieving sufficient precision and stability can they be gradually granted control permissions in stages.

 

For global markets, where overseas vehicle software safety reviews are stringent, this closed-loop safety system is also a mandatory qualification for automakers globalization. Model capability is only the starting point of innovation—safety, reliability, and verifiability are the keys to global commercialization.

 

 

Replicating the Smartphone Subscription Model Is a Strategic Misjudgment for AI Cockpit Commercialization

Global Auto: In recent years, many automakers have begun exploring feature subscriptions and monthly payment models. However, you believe that "directly copying smartphone subscription models for AI Cockpit commercialization is a pseudo-proposition." What's the logic behind this judgment?

Wu Yongqiao: Directly copying the smartphone subscription models for AI cockpit commercialization fundamentally misjudge the nature of automotive consumption——it simply does not work.

 

With the rise of the software-defined vehicle concept, many automakers hope to learn from consumer electronics by creating recurring revenue streams through feature subscriptions and software unlocks. However, there are fundamental differences between cars and smartphones in product attributes, usage frequency, and consumer psychology, and user behavior. User behavior patterns in China and overseas markets cannot be simply compared.

 

Smartphones are high-frequency interactive consumer goods. Users interact dozens or even hundreds of times daily. Subscription services such as memberships and cloud storage can continuously generate perceived value. Cars, on the other hand, are long-cycle durable consumer goods. Consumers pay tens of thousands of yuan (RMB) when purchasing a vehicle. Thus, consumers naturally have the perception that "most functions are already included in the purchasing price."

 

More importantly, consumers are never willing to pay for software themselves; they only pay for the tangible, sustained value that software delivers. For instance, the navigation system itself does not create value; what truly matters is the tangible experiences which the navigation system delivers: real-time traffic updates, parking recommendations, cross-city route planning, and scenario-based route optimization. Similarly, once a large model is deployed in a vehicle, users are not concerned with model parameters, but rather whether the model can save time, reduce costs, and improve driving efficiency.

 

Therefore, automotive commercialization must align with car owners' long-cycle, experience-oriented consumption habits. The commercialization needs to root in mobility services, and adapt to different user habits across global markets, to build a truly sustainable profit model.

 

Emotional Interaction Is a “Trojan horse for privacy”: AI Cockpit Experience Must Not Come at The Cost of Privacy

 

Global Auto: Vehicles are becoming increasingly intelligent about their users. AI Cockpits are able to accurately recognize user emotions, facial expressions, and vocal states, making the experience more human-centric. Yet you have described such emotional interaction as a "Trojan Horse for privacy" How do you interpret the risks involved?

 

Wu Yongqiao: The more an AI Cockpit "understands the user," the blurrier the boundaries of data collection become, and the higher the risk of privacy leakage. So-called emotional interaction is a transaction that trades privacy for AI Cockpit experience.

 

Emotional interaction and multimodal perception require the collection of highly sensitive personal data: voice content, facial expressions, body gestures, emotional states, and even in-car conversations and behavioral patterns. The sensitivity of this data is far greater than location or navigation data.

 

Future AI Cockpits may recognize the moment you enter the car and determine that “you are in a bad mood today,” then proactively play soothing music; detect fatigue when you yawn repeatedly and suggest taking a break; switch to child mode when a child enters the vehicle; or even predict your destination in advance based on long-term observation of travel patterns. These experiences may seem thoughtful; the premise is that the vehicle must continuously sense and record a large amount of personal information.

 

The industry faces two major vulnerabilities: First, user authorization is a mere formality—lengthy privacy agreement and obscure jargon make "click to agree" the default action. Users are unaware of what data is being collected and for what purpose. Second, Second, many automakers upload perception data to the cloud for processing, creating centralized repositories of highly sensitive information. Once cloud security is compromised, it can easily trigger large-scale privacy breaches. This is what I refer to as a "Trojan Horse for Privacy": features that appear to enhance user experience may carry hidden privacy risks.

 

Major markets such as Europe and North America are extremely strict to privacy regulations. Bosch solution is to deploy models On-Device, ensuring that user privacy does not go to the cloud. In simple terms, all emotional recognition, voice interaction, and facial perception are processed locally within the vehicle, and raw sensitive data remains entirely inside the car.

 

The best form of privacy protection is to ensure that sensitive data never leaves the vehicle or gets uploaded to the cloud. Humanized AI Cockpit experiences must never come at the expense of user privacy. On-Device Processing is the most practical solution for privacy and security.

 

Breaking the “Impossible Triangle” of On-Device Large Models Through Hardware–Software Synergy

Global Auto: The industry is making a strong push toward On-Device large models, yet most players remain constrained by the three factors of "experience, speed, and cost"—the so called “impossible trinity.” You have identified memory and algorithm quality as key levers for overcoming this triangle. Could you elaborate?

 

Wu Yongqiao: Many believe that on-device large models cannot simultaneously optimize "experience, speed, and cost". The root cause of this contradiction has never been insufficient computing power—it is data bandwidth that has become the bottleneck.

 

No matter how powerful an in-vehicle chip may be, if memory bandwidth is insufficient, the massive model data and real-time perception data can't be scheduled efficiently. As a result, computing resources remain underutilized, leading to latency and lag in AI interactions, especially in weak-network environments. The adoption of high-bandwidth memory addresses this fundamental bottleneck at its source.

 

Hardware fills the gaps, algorithms improve efficiency. Together, they can completely unlock the bottlenecks of On-Device large models.

 

First, sufficient bandwidth enables on-device large models to run at full speed, significantly improving response time and eliminating stuttering and latency issues. Second, automakers no longer rely solely on the most advanced, high-cost computing chips. Algorithmic optimization enables mid-range compute chips to support high-performance large models, effectively controlling overall hardware costs. Finally, once cost and speed are effectively addressed, developers can fully optimize model algorithms, enrich interaction scenarios, and enhance user experience.

 

The so-called "impossible trinity" of the past was a consequence of immature hardware conditions. With the continuous evolution of hardware-software synergy, the leap of On-Device large models is only just beginning. The balance of experience, speed, and cost is transitioning to reality.

 

 

 

 

Competing on Computing Power Alone Is Meaningless; User Value Is the Ultimate Benchmark

Global Auto: The industry often promotes hundreds or even thousands of TOPs in computing power. Yet you believe much of this computing power competition is just marketing noise. Why are you skeptical about computing power racing?

 

Wu Yongqiao: Computing power figures measured in hundreds or thousands of TOPs are marketing metrics aimed at industry rather than experiences that users can perceive. Computing power is an entry ticket, not the core competitiveness that determines user experience.

 

Sitting inside a vehicle, ordinary users can't perceive a chip's benchmark scores. They feel directly three things: whether voice commands are accurately understood, whether features invocation is easy and intuitive to use, and whether these features continue to work in weak or no-network conditions.

 

Today, is overly focused on competing chip performance figures and using high computing power as a marketing selling point, while neglecting the algorithm optimization and scenario refinement that truly define the user experience. No matter how powerful cloud computing is, the moment a vehicle enters an underground garage or remote area with weak or no network, cloud services fail and the AI Cockpit experience decline. Chasing computing power without regard for real-world driving scenarios is an unproductive race and marketing noise.

 

Great in-vehicle AI is never built by stacking computing power; it's refined through real-world scenarios. The industry must move beyond computing power race and focus on refining high-frequency scenarios and improving algorithmic efficiency: First, prioritize algorithm optimization—improve model compression, instruction recognition, and multi-task coordination efficiency on existing computing power, putting every unit of computing power to use. Second, focus on high-frequency daily scenarios, making basic features extremely stable, smooth, and user-friendly. Third, develop the end-cloud collaborative architecture, with particular emphasis on On-Device standalone operation capability in weak-network and no-network scenarios, ensuring consistent experience.

 

Automobiles are industrial products that must serve millions of users in real-world driving environments. Computing power competition detached from user needs is meaningless. Focusing technology on user-perceptible experiences is the direction for healthy industry development.

 

Conclusion

As Chinese automakers accelerate their global expansion, regulatory frameworks, data governance requirements, and user behaviors across different markets are becoming the ultimate test of AI Cockpit products. Building a world-class AI cockpit requires deep expertise in the domestic market and the ability to deliver a consistent balance of user experience, safety, and compliance on a global scale.

 

Only by moving beyond hype and focusing on safety, user value, and commercial viability can intelligent vehicles become users’ trusted mobility companions. The transformation of the global automotive industry is only just beginning.
